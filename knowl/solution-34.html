Solution 4.2.3.5.1.

We can begin by noticing that if we look only at the behavior of the summand at infinity, then we have a summand that looks like \(\frac{k}{k\sqrt{k}}=\frac{k}{k^{1.5}}=\frac{1}{k^{0.5}}\text{.}\) This makes the series a candidate for using TheoremÂ 4.2.5. Let \(b_k=\frac{1}{k^{0.5}}\text{,}\) then
\begin{align*} \lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{a_k}{b_k} \amp =\lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{\frac{k-2}{k\sqrt{k}}}{\frac{1}{k^{0.5}}} \\ \amp = \lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{\sqrt{k}(k-2)}{k\sqrt{k}} \\ \amp = 1 \end{align*}
Therefore, since the limit is a positive real number, and the series \(\sum_k b_k\) diverges, then so does the given series.
