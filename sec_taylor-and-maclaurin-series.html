    Section 5.2 Tylor and Maclaurin Series

    Theorem 5.2.1.

    Let \(f\) be an \(n\)-times differentiable function where all of the derivatives of \(f\) are defined at a value \(x_0=c\text{.}\) Then we can express the function \(f\) as a series by
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=\sum_{k=0}^\infty\frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-c)^k \end{equation*}
    Although it looks a little complicated, the idea and process of calculating a Taylor series requires a bit of computation, but a lot of patter recognition. To start, it is easier to look at what is known as a Taylor Polynoimal, which is just a finite number of terms of the series, rather than the infinite series itself. To that end, consider the following example.

    Example 5.2.2.

    Construct a third-degree Taylor polynomial of \(f(x)=e^x\) centered at \(c=1\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    To construct the third-degree Taylor Polynomial for the function, it is perhaps best to consider a table of values that calculates \(\displaystyle f^{(k)}(c)\text{,}\) \(k!\) to easily determine the value of \(\displaystyle \frac{f^{(k)}(c)}{k!}\text{.}\) These will be the coefficients of the polynomial.
    \(k\) \(f^{(k)}(c)\) \(k!\) \(\displaystyle \frac{f^{(k)}(c)}{k!}\)
    \(0\) \(e^1\) \(1\) \(\frac{e}{1}\)
    \(0\) \(e^1\) \(1\) \(\frac{e}{1}\)
    \(0\) \(e^1\) \(2\) \(\frac{e}{2}\)
    \(0\) \(e^1\) \(6\) \(\frac{e}{6}\)
    Using this table, we can assemble the polynomial to be
    \begin{equation*} P_4(x)=e+e(x-1)+\frac{e}{1}(x-1)^2+\frac{e}{6}(x-1)^3 \end{equation*}
